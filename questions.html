  1. What is agile testing?
    • Testing done within the team
    • Ideally automated as part of CI / CD pipeline
    • Test Pyramid
    • Unit Tests
    • Functional Tests
    • Non Functional
    • Integration Tests
    • Smoke/E2E tests
    • Eploratory tests
    • TDD
  2. How is agile methodology different than traditional waterfall process?
    • Watefall is:
      • lots of upfront planning
      • No scope for change, if there is it's a change control process
      • Hand off between all the different phases - analysis, development, testing
      • Ghant Charts
      • No deviations from the plan
      • Large Big Bang releases
      • Large testing phase at the end, including UAT cycles with stakeholders etc
    • Agile is:
      • Iterations
      • Features delivered often - elease to production early and often with a working solution
      • Smaller releases - less risj
      • Early feedback from stakeholders as they and other parts of business are involved from start
      • Inspect, adapt
      • Testing done as you go
  3. What are pros and cons of agile methodology?
    • Pros:
      • Working software in production early followed by continuous delivery
      • Early Feedback from Users
      • Fail fast
      • Can pivot more easily if requried
      • Concentrate on the risky areas/uncertainty first
      • Can inspect and adapt to make things better (e.g. work more efficiently or go in a bteer direction from the stakeholder
      • Proving out parts of the pipeline to production early rather than at the end
      • Developers like this approach better than waterfall
      • If you have the correct engineering culture devs will have more buy in and ownership
      • Emergent design
      • Close interaction between customer, devs and testers throughout
    • Cons:
      • Needs buy in from the top if it is truly to be successful
      • Could be tricky in a vendor - client relationship scenario where fixed price is used (depends on the billing model)
      • They still require lots of upfront analysis
      • Hard to predict overall size of the project up front if you don't go into detail
      • Long Term planning can be difficult to get developers to do
      • Needs lots of effort to co-ordinate with other teams if it is at scale
      • Can be a lot of meetings
      • Devs may think some of the planning and estimation tools are a waste of time/going through the 15-must-know-agile-methodology-interview-questions
      • If using Kanban hard to predict/report

  6. Difference between extreme programming and scrum?
    • Extreme Programming (seems to a bit like what we do in GZ & Sonic). Devs work in a Kanban fashion but strictly take the top item off of the backlog as piroritised by PO/Customer
    • Scrum has sprints which are planned and fixed and devs decide what order they want to pick off items from the backlog during that sprint.
  7. Can you explain lean methodology in detail?
    • Just in Time
    • AIM - Increasing speed of dev and decreasing cost
    • Reduce non value add activities
    • Agile itself is more of a lean method of Software Delivery
    • Add Value. Learn what is valuable by delivering quickly and getting feedback.
    • Test ideas at target market before throwing heavy money at it.
    • Lightweight Leadership - Lean leadership empowers employees with the autonomy to make decisions, the opportunity to master their craft, and the purpose (the “why” behind the work) to understand the value of their efforts. The role of the leader is to define the goal at hand, and then allow their talented employees to discover the most appropriate course of action toward that goal.
    • Avoid excessive planning, context switching, wait times between steps
  8. What is Kanban?
    • WIP limits
    • One large prioritised backlog
  9. Is there any difference between incremental and iterative development?
    • Interative (thin slice): fixed cycles of sprints and releases are iterated through until final product at end. Release basic functionality - thin slice and layer extra functionality on top in future iterations.
    • Incremental (functionality): incrementally adding functionality (tested and developed) to prod and incrementing on that in terms of func while maintaining what you have on prod. Idea is you don't need to revisit the work of that iteration again.
    • Both are used in Agile
  10. What are burndown and burn-up charts in agile methodology?
    • Burndown - Report on how much work is remaining (amt of points left). 2 Lines, expected remaining v actual remaining
    • Burnup - Report of planned tasks that are completed. 3 lines - scope, expected progress and actual progress
    • Both used to communicate with stakeholders
